A lot of modern medicine is focused on **death, cancer, dementia, heart disease, infectious disease, and visible crisis**, while many female-specific burdens are treated as “normal life,” “women’s issues,” or “quality-of-life problems.” That tells us something important about society.

It does **not** mean nothing has progressed. Maternal mortality, contraception, fertility treatment, safer childbirth, cervical cancer screening, breast cancer treatment, and reproductive medicine have improved enormously. But compared with the scale of the burden, areas like menstrual pain, endometriosis, menopause, heavy bleeding, pelvic pain, pregnancy-related long-term damage, and hormonal health have often been under-researched and under-discussed.

## The deeper issue: medicine often prioritises survival over suffering

Alzheimer’s, cancer, and dementia get attention partly because they are catastrophic, fatal, expensive, and affect both men and women. Society is naturally frightened by death and cognitive collapse.

But many women’s biological burdens are different. They may be chronic, recurring, private, painful, embarrassing to discuss, and not always fatal. So they get downgraded.

That reflects a hidden value judgment:

> If a condition does not kill you, society often treats it as less urgent — even if it reduces your life every month, every year, for decades.

This is one reason women’s health has been historically underfunded. A 2024 National Academies/NCBI report examined gaps in NIH funding for conditions specific to women, more common in women, or affecting women differently. ([NCBI][1]) Nature also described women’s health research as long underfunded, partly because of stigma around conditions mainly affecting women. ([Nature][2])

## It is partly a “men’s world,” but not only that

It is fair to say male-dominated institutions shaped medicine for a long time. Medical research historically used male bodies as the default model. Female biology was often treated as a special case, complicated by hormones, pregnancy, menstruation, and reproductive cycles.

That creates a deep bias: the male body becomes “standard,” and the female body becomes “variation.”

But it is not only a simple conspiracy of men ignoring women. Several forces combine:

First, female biology is genuinely complex. Hormonal cycles, pregnancy, childbirth, postpartum changes, menopause, autoimmune differences, pain perception, and reproductive health create complicated research problems.

Second, many women’s conditions are not immediately fatal, so funding systems give them lower priority.

Third, stigma matters. Menstruation, pelvic pain, menopause, sexual pain, infertility, pregnancy injury, and bleeding are often culturally uncomfortable topics.

Fourth, many women themselves are socialised to endure. They may be told pain is normal, mood changes are normal, childbirth damage is normal, menopause is normal, and therefore not something requiring serious innovation.

So yes, patriarchy or male-centered culture is part of the story. But the full story is: **male-default science + stigma + nonfatal suffering + institutional inertia + women being expected to endure.**

## The “50% of humanity” point is very strong

Your point is especially important because these are not rare edge cases. Menstruation, pregnancy, childbirth, menopause, and female hormonal transitions affect a huge part of humanity.

Endometriosis alone is estimated to affect around **190 million women and girls globally**, yet it has historically been underfunded and often involves long diagnostic delays. ([Nature][3]) Menopause is experienced by roughly half of humanity, but Nature reported in 2025 that menopause research remains globally underfunded. ([Nature][4])

So the issue is not that the problems are small. The issue is that society has often classified them as “ordinary female suffering” rather than as major medical and technological frontiers.

## What does this say about society?

It says society often defines “important problems” through the eyes of power.

If the people setting the agenda are mostly men, older elites, institutions, investors, military priorities, insurance systems, and prestige scientists, then the problems that receive attention are the ones that look urgent to them: death, war, cancer, productivity loss, ageing elites, national competition, and expensive hospital diseases.

But pain that happens privately in bedrooms, bathrooms, clinics, pregnancies, and daily life may not become a grand civilisational problem — even when it affects hundreds of millions.

That is a moral failure, but also an imagination failure.

A Thiel-like critique would be even sharper: society claims to be technologically advanced, but it has normalised huge zones of suffering. It can build AI models and rockets, but many basic female biological burdens are still treated with crude tools, delayed diagnosis, or “just live with it” attitudes.

## Has it progressed from a few hundred years ago?

Yes, but unevenly.

Compared with a few hundred years ago, the progress is massive: safer childbirth, antibiotics, blood transfusion, C-sections, anaesthesia, contraception, ultrasound, IVF, cancer screening, and maternal care are enormous advances.

But compared with what modern science should be capable of, progress is disappointing in many areas.

For example, many women still struggle to get clear answers for severe menstrual pain, endometriosis, menopause symptoms, chronic pelvic pain, postpartum issues, and hormonal problems. The fact that these are still often dismissed suggests that the old attitude has not fully disappeared. It has become more polite, more medicalised, and more bureaucratic, but the deeper assumption can remain:

> “This is part of being a woman.”

That assumption is exactly what should be challenged.

## Are these problems unsolvable?

No. Some are difficult, but “difficult” is not the same as “unsolvable.”

There are several solvable or improvable areas:

Better pain control.
Better diagnostics for endometriosis and pelvic disorders.
Better non-invasive tests.
Better understanding of hormonal changes.
Better menopause care.
Better contraception with fewer side effects.
Better postpartum recovery medicine.
Better workplace and school accommodation.
Better sex-specific drug testing.
Better data collection by sex and life stage.

The women’s health gap is increasingly being framed as both a health and economic issue. McKinsey and the World Economic Forum estimated that closing the women’s health gap could add around **$1 trillion annually** to the global economy by 2040, and noted that women spend about **25% more of their lives in poor health** than men. ([McKinsey & Company][5])

So the problem is not lack of value. It is lack of prioritisation.

## Why popular culture does not discuss it enough

Popular culture discusses women’s bodies constantly, but often in the wrong way: beauty, fertility, sexuality, ageing appearance, weight, motherhood identity. It is less comfortable discussing female pain as a serious engineering and medical problem.

That itself is revealing.

Society is often more interested in how women’s bodies look, perform, reproduce, or remain attractive than in how much women’s bodies suffer.

That is a harsh sentence, but I think it captures part of your discomfort.

## The Thiel-style interpretation

If we apply the interview’s worldview, the critique would be:

Modern society is not as advanced as it thinks. It has accepted many inherited biological limits as normal, especially when those limits affect women. It funds prestigious diseases and dramatic moonshots, but it neglects ordinary recurring suffering because it lacks imagination, courage, and institutional seriousness.

A Thiel-like person might say:

> “A society that truly believed in progress would not accept that half of humanity must simply endure predictable biological pain for decades.”

He would probably treat women’s health not as a soft social issue, but as a frontier technology problem.

## My balanced conclusion

It is not correct to say there has been no progress. There has been huge progress. It is also not correct to say these problems are naturally unsolvable. Many are under-solved because they were under-studied, under-funded, and culturally normalised.

So yes, part of it is that the world has historically been organised around male bodies and male priorities. But the deeper issue is broader: society often ignores suffering that is common, private, nonfatal, and borne by people expected to endure it. Women’s biological pain falls exactly into that neglected category.

A truly future-oriented society would treat this as one of the great unfinished medical frontiers.
